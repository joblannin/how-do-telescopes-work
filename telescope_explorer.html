Refractor Telescope

A refractor uses glass lenses to bend (refract) light and bring it to a focus. Light enters through a large objective lens at the front, travels down the tube, and converges at a focal point before reaching your eye through the eyepiece. Refractors are great for viewing the Moon, planets, and double stars.

Objective Lens
Tap to reveal
The large glass lens at the front of the telescope. It gathers light from distant objects and bends it inward, bringing it to a focus. The wider the lens, the more light it collects.
Focal Point
Tap to reveal
The spot where all the light rays converge after passing through the objective lens. The distance from the lens to this point is called the focal length — longer focal lengths give more magnification.
Eyepiece
Tap to reveal
A small, removable lens you look through. It acts like a magnifying glass, spreading out the focused light so your eye can see a wide, magnified image. Swapping eyepieces changes the magnification.
Tube & Focuser
Tap to reveal
The tube keeps stray light out and holds everything in alignment. The focuser is a rack-and-pinion or Crayford mechanism that slides the eyepiece in or out until the image is sharp.

Reflector Telescope (10" Dobsonian)

A reflector uses mirrors instead of lenses. Light travels down the open tube, hits a large curved primary mirror at the bottom, and bounces back up to a small secondary mirror near the top. The secondary deflects the light out the side of the tube into the eyepiece. This design — a Newtonian reflector on a Dobsonian mount — is one of the most popular for backyard astronomy because it gives a large mirror at low cost.

Primary Mirror
Tap to reveal
The large curved (parabolic) mirror at the bottom of the tube. At 10 inches (254 mm) across, it collects a huge amount of light — about 1,600 times more than the human eye — and reflects it upward to a point.
Secondary Mirror
Tap to reveal
A small, flat mirror held in the centre of the tube by a metal "spider". It sits at 45° and deflects the converging light sideways out through the focuser hole in the tube wall.
Eyepiece & Focuser
Tap to reveal
The eyepiece magnifies the focused image. The focuser on the side of the tube racks the eyepiece in or out to sharpen the image, just like in a refractor.
Dobsonian Mount
Tap to reveal
A simple, low-cost alt-azimuth mount made of a rocker box sitting on a rotating ground board. It moves up-down (altitude) and left-right (azimuth). No motors needed — you simply push the tube to point at objects.

Refractor vs Reflector

Both types collect light and magnify distant objects, but they work differently and each has its own strengths. Tap each card to reveal more.
🔬 Refractor
  • Uses lenses
  • Sealed tube — low maintenance
  • Sharp, high-contrast views
  • Great for Moon & planets
  • Gets expensive at large sizes
  • Can cause colour fringing (chromatic aberration)
🔮 Reflector (Dobsonian)
  • Uses mirrors
  • No colour fringing
  • Large aperture at low cost
  • Superb for deep-sky objects
  • Mirrors need occasional realignment (collimation)
  • Open tube can let in dust

Key Concepts — tap to reveal
Aperture
Tap to reveal
The diameter of the main lens or mirror. Bigger aperture = more light collected = fainter objects visible. A 10" mirror collects about 4× as much light as a 5" mirror.
Focal Length
Tap to reveal
The distance from the lens/mirror to the focal point. Longer focal length = higher magnification at a given eyepiece, but narrower field of view.
Magnification
Tap to reveal
Calculated as: telescope focal length ÷ eyepiece focal length. A 1200mm telescope with a 10mm eyepiece gives 120× magnification. Higher is not always better — too much magnification makes the image dim and blurry.
Refraction vs Reflection
Tap to reveal
Refraction is when light bends as it passes through glass — different colours bend by slightly different amounts, which can cause rainbow fringes. Reflection bounces all colours equally off a mirror, so there is no colour fringing.
